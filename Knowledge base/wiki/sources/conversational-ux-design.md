## Key takeaways

### 1) Conversation type diversity is real (design for multiple modes)

[[The 6 Types of Conversations with Generative AI]] argues there is **no single optimal conversation length**. People use bots via distinct interaction patterns, including “search query,” “funneling,” “exploring,” “chiseling,” “expanding,” and “pinpointing.” Good UX supports switching between these patterns and reduces the burden of query articulation (e.g., via helping questions and suggested followups).

### 2) Chatbots break when users deviate from linear flows

[[The User Experience of Chatbots]] reports that most real-world bots are thin decision trees: they feel smooth only when users provide “legal” answers. When inputs deviate, bots fail recovery and often force restarts. The UX implication is not “make the bot more human,” but **make boundaries explicit** and provide strong escape hatches.

### 3) Polyadic conversational agents add social-boundary risks

The CHI’22 review emphasizes that polyadic agents (mediating human–human interactions) require careful design around **privacy, disclosure, and identification**, plus stronger trust-building and usability evaluation methods. It synthesizes effects into communication, engagement, connection, and relationship maintenance.
