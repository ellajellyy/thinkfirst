# The Mom Test

**Raw file:** `raw/the-mom-test-by-rob-fitzpatrick.md`  
**Date ingested:** 2026-04-24  
**Author:** Rob Fitzpatrick (2013)  
**Relevance to Think First:** A practical handbook for extracting *truth* (not validation) from customer conversations—directly aligned with Think First’s “understand before you build” principle.

---

## Summary

The book argues that early customer conversations are **bad by default** because founders accidentally ask questions that invite *compliments, hypotheticals, and future promises*—all of which create dangerous false positives. Its core remedy is “The Mom Test”: ask questions that even someone biased to be nice (your mom) **can’t lie** about because you’re asking about **their real past behavior** and **current constraints**, not your idea.

In practice, it’s a set of interviewing rules and tactics for:\n
- avoiding “idea validation” traps,\n
- digging into concrete past examples,\n
- learning purchase processes and budgets,\n
- and escalating from talk to *commitment* (pre-orders, intros, time on calendar) as evidence.

---

## Key Claims

1. **Opinions are worthless** for deciding what to build; you need facts from the customer’s lived experience.\n
2. **Anything involving the future is an over-optimistic lie**; ask about what happened (past) and what’s happening (now).\n
3. The highest-value early conversations produce **concrete facts** about customers’ lives and workflows, not feedback on your pitch.\n
4. **Compliments, fluff, and ideas** are the three common forms of bad data; you must actively deflect and anchor conversations back to reality.\n
5. Good questions are often “show me” questions: *“Talk me through the last time that happened.”*\n
